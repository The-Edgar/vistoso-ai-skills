# Style Guide: Artistic Vocabulary for Image Generation

Comprehensive reference of artistic terms, styles, and techniques to enhance prompt quality.

## Photography Terminology

### Shot Types
- **Extreme Close-Up (ECU)**: Fills frame with a single feature (eye, lips, object detail)
- **Close-Up**: Head and shoulders, intimate and detailed
- **Medium Close-Up**: Chest and up, common for dialogue and expression
- **Medium Shot**: Waist and up, shows subject and some context
- **Medium Long Shot**: Knees and up, includes more environment
- **Long Shot**: Full body visible, establishing character in space
- **Extreme Long Shot**: Subject small in frame, emphasizes environment
- **Macro Shot**: Extreme magnification of tiny details
- **Aerial/Drone Shot**: Bird's eye view from above

### Camera Angles
- **Eye Level**: Neutral, natural perspective
- **High Angle**: Camera looking down, makes subject appear vulnerable/small
- **Low Angle**: Camera looking up, makes subject appear powerful/heroic
- **Dutch Angle (Canted)**: Tilted horizon, creates tension/unease
- **Overhead/Bird's Eye**: Directly above, flattens space
- **Worm's Eye**: Extremely low angle looking up
- **Over-the-Shoulder**: Frame includes foreground shoulder/head
- **Point of View (POV)**: Shows what character sees

### Lens Characteristics
- **Wide-Angle (14-35mm)**: Expansive view, exaggerated perspective, architectural
- **Standard (50mm)**: Natural perspective, mimics human vision
- **Portrait (85mm)**: Flattering compression, beautiful bokeh
- **Telephoto (100-300mm)**: Compressed perspective, isolates subject
- **Macro**: Extreme close-up magnification of tiny subjects
- **Fisheye**: Ultra-wide with spherical distortion
- **Tilt-Shift**: Miniature effect or perspective correction

### Depth of Field
- **Shallow DOF (f/1.4-f/2.8)**: Blurred background, subject isolation, bokeh
- **Medium DOF (f/4-f/8)**: Partial background detail
- **Deep DOF (f/11-f/22)**: Everything sharp, landscape photography

### Focus Techniques
- **Bokeh**: Quality of out-of-focus areas, often circular light blurs
- **Rack Focus**: Shift focus from foreground to background
- **Selective Focus**: One element sharp, rest blurred
- **Soft Focus**: Dreamy, slightly diffused overall
- **Tack Sharp**: Perfectly crisp and clear

### Motion Effects
- **Motion Blur**: Subject moving, creates sense of speed
- **Panning**: Camera follows moving subject, background blurred
- **Freeze Frame**: Action stopped, every detail sharp
- **Long Exposure**: Smooth water, light trails, motion streaks
- **High-Speed Photography**: Captures split-second moments

## Lighting Terminology

### Natural Light
- **Golden Hour**: Hour after sunrise/before sunset, warm soft light
- **Blue Hour**: Twilight before sunrise/after sunset, cool blue tones
- **Midday Sun**: Harsh overhead light, strong shadows
- **Overcast**: Soft, diffused, shadowless light
- **Dappled Light**: Filtered through leaves, creating patterns
- **Backlighting**: Light from behind subject, creates rim light/silhouette
- **Side Lighting**: Light from side, emphasizes texture and form
- **Window Light**: Soft directional light, often flattering for portraits

### Studio Lighting Setups
- **Key Light**: Main light source, defines subject
- **Fill Light**: Softens shadows from key light
- **Back Light/Rim Light**: Separates subject from background
- **Three-Point Lighting**: Key + Fill + Back, classic setup
- **Butterfly Lighting**: Light from above, creates butterfly shadow under nose
- **Rembrandt Lighting**: Triangle of light on cheek, dramatic
- **Split Lighting**: Half face lit, half in shadow, dramatic
- **Loop Lighting**: Small nose shadow loops to side, flattering

### Lighting Quality
- **Hard Light**: Direct, creates defined shadows (sun, spotlight)
- **Soft Light**: Diffused, gentle shadows (overcast, softbox)
- **Dramatic Lighting**: High contrast, deep shadows
- **Even Lighting**: Uniform, minimal shadows
- **Moody Lighting**: Low key, predominantly dark tones
- **High Key**: Predominantly bright, minimal shadows
- **Low Key**: Predominantly dark, dramatic

### Light Direction
- **Front Lighting**: Flat, reduces texture
- **Side Lighting**: Enhances texture and dimension
- **Top Lighting**: Overhead, can be dramatic or harsh
- **Bottom Lighting**: Unnatural, eerie effect
- **Edge/Rim Lighting**: Outlines subject, creates separation

### Color Temperature
- **Warm (2700-3500K)**: Golden, cozy, sunset, incandescent
- **Neutral (4000-5000K)**: Daylight, balanced
- **Cool (5500-7000K)**: Blue tones, shadow areas, overcast
- **Mixed Temperature**: Combining warm and cool for contrast

## Art Styles and Movements

### Classical/Traditional
- **Renaissance**: Realistic, humanistic, balanced composition, chiaroscuro
- **Baroque**: Dramatic, ornate, dynamic movement, theatrical lighting
- **Romanticism**: Emotional, dramatic landscapes, sublime nature
- **Realism**: Accurate representation, everyday subjects, detailed
- **Academic Art**: Classical technique, idealized forms, historical subjects

### Modern Movements
- **Impressionism**: Loose brushwork, light effects, color over line (Monet, Renoir)
- **Post-Impressionism**: Symbolic, geometric, expressive color (Van Gogh, Cézanne)
- **Expressionism**: Distorted reality, emotional intensity, bold colors
- **Cubism**: Geometric shapes, multiple perspectives (Picasso, Braque)
- **Surrealism**: Dreamlike, illogical, subconscious (Dalí, Magritte)
- **Abstract Expressionism**: Non-representational, gestural, emotional
- **Pop Art**: Commercial imagery, bold colors, mass culture (Warhol, Lichtenstein)
- **Minimalism**: Stripped down, essential elements, geometric
- **Art Nouveau**: Organic lines, decorative, natural forms

### Contemporary Styles
- **Hyperrealism**: Extremely detailed, photo-like precision
- **Photorealism**: Paintings that look like photographs
- **Neo-Pop**: Modern pop art, digital culture references
- **Street Art**: Urban, graffiti-inspired, bold graphics
- **Digital Art**: Created with digital tools, varied substyles
- **Low Poly**: Geometric, faceted, 3D aesthetic
- **Glitch Art**: Digital errors as aesthetic, distortion
- **Vaporwave**: Nostalgic, neon, 80s/90s aesthetic, surreal

### Illustration Styles
- **Cartoon**: Simplified, exaggerated, playful
- **Anime/Manga**: Japanese animation style, large eyes, stylized
- **Comic Book**: Bold outlines, flat colors, action-oriented
- **Sketch**: Rough, unfinished, loose lines
- **Watercolor**: Transparent layers, soft edges, fluid
- **Ink Drawing**: Bold lines, crosshatching, high contrast
- **Flat Design**: Minimalist, 2D, solid colors, simple shapes
- **Isometric**: 3D objects in 2D, technical, game-like

### Specialty Styles
- **Film Noir**: High contrast black and white, dramatic shadows
- **Cyberpunk**: Futuristic, neon, dystopian, tech-heavy
- **Steampunk**: Victorian + industrial, brass and gears
- **Art Deco**: Geometric, luxurious, symmetrical, 1920s-30s
- **Mid-Century Modern**: Clean lines, organic curves, retro
- **Brutalism**: Raw concrete, geometric, imposing
- **Memphis Design**: Bold patterns, bright colors, geometric, 1980s

## Color and Tone

### Color Palettes
- **Monochromatic**: Variations of single hue
- **Analogous**: Adjacent colors on wheel (harmonious)
- **Complementary**: Opposite colors (high contrast)
- **Triadic**: Three evenly spaced colors (balanced)
- **Split-Complementary**: Base + two adjacent to complement
- **Tetradic**: Two complementary pairs

### Color Moods
- **Warm Palette**: Reds, oranges, yellows - energetic, welcoming
- **Cool Palette**: Blues, greens, purples - calm, professional
- **Earthy Tones**: Browns, tans, greens - natural, grounded
- **Pastel**: Soft, desaturated - gentle, youthful
- **Vibrant**: Saturated, intense - bold, attention-grabbing
- **Muted**: Desaturated, subtle - sophisticated, understated
- **Neon**: Bright, fluorescent - energetic, modern, electric

### Tonal Qualities
- **High Contrast**: Strong difference between lights and darks
- **Low Contrast**: Subtle differences, soft transitions
- **High Saturation**: Pure, vivid colors
- **Desaturated**: Reduced color intensity, faded
- **High Key**: Predominantly light tones
- **Low Key**: Predominantly dark tones
- **Sepia Tone**: Warm brown monochrome, vintage feel
- **Black and White**: Timeless, focuses on form and contrast

## Compositional Techniques

### Classic Rules
- **Rule of Thirds**: Divide frame into 9 squares, place key elements at intersections
- **Golden Ratio**: Spiral composition, mathematically pleasing proportions
- **Leading Lines**: Lines guide eye through image
- **Symmetry**: Balanced, mirror-like composition
- **Asymmetry**: Dynamic, creates tension and interest
- **Frame Within Frame**: Use environmental elements to frame subject
- **Negative Space**: Empty space emphasizes subject
- **Fill the Frame**: Subject dominates, minimal background

### Visual Flow
- **S-Curve**: Graceful, flowing composition
- **Diagonal Lines**: Dynamic, energetic
- **Triangular Composition**: Stable, hierarchical
- **Circular Composition**: Contained, focuses attention
- **Radial**: Elements emanate from center
- **Pattern and Repetition**: Creates rhythm and interest
- **Breaking Pattern**: One different element draws eye

### Perspective
- **Linear Perspective**: Converging lines to vanishing point
- **One-Point Perspective**: Single vanishing point, symmetrical
- **Two-Point Perspective**: Two vanishing points, angular
- **Three-Point Perspective**: Includes vertical convergence
- **Forced Perspective**: Optical illusion of size/distance
- **Flat/Isometric**: No perspective, parallel lines

## Material and Texture Vocabulary

### Surface Qualities
- **Glossy/Shiny**: Reflective, smooth, lustrous
- **Matte**: Non-reflective, flat finish
- **Brushed**: Subtle linear texture (metal)
- **Polished**: Smooth, refined, mirror-like
- **Rough**: Uneven, tactile, coarse
- **Smooth**: Even, sleek surface
- **Textured**: Visible, tactile surface variation
- **Weathered**: Aged, worn, showing use
- **Pristine**: Perfect, new, unblemished

### Material Types
- **Metallic**: Reflective, conductive appearance (gold, silver, copper, steel)
- **Glass**: Transparent, refractive, reflective
- **Fabric**: Woven, draped, shows folds (cotton, silk, wool, linen)
- **Leather**: Organic, textured, ages with patina
- **Wood**: Grained, warm, natural variation (oak, walnut, pine)
- **Stone**: Heavy, textured, natural (marble, granite, slate)
- **Ceramic**: Smooth, glazed or matte, fragile appearance
- **Plastic**: Smooth, uniform, modern (glossy or matte)
- **Paper**: Fibrous, matte, absorbs light

### Transparency
- **Transparent**: See-through, clear (glass, water)
- **Translucent**: Diffuses light, partially see-through (frosted glass, thin fabric)
- **Opaque**: Blocks light completely, solid
- **Semi-Transparent**: Partially visible through

### Light Interaction
- **Reflective**: Mirrors surroundings
- **Refractive**: Bends light (glass, water, gems)
- **Subsurface Scattering**: Light penetrates and diffuses (wax, skin, marble)
- **Iridescent**: Color changes with viewing angle (oil slick, soap bubble)
- **Metallic**: Reflects with colored tint
- **Pearlescent**: Soft, lustrous sheen
- **Velvet**: Absorbs light, rich appearance

## Technical Specifications

### Print Specifications
- **Resolution**: 300 DPI minimum for print
- **Color Space**: CMYK for print, RGB for digital
- **Bleed**: 3mm beyond trim for safe printing
- **Safe Zone**: Keep text 5mm inside trim

### Digital Specifications
- **Resolution**: 72-96 DPI for screens
- **File Format**: PNG for transparency, JPG for photos, SVG for vectors
- **Optimization**: Compress for web without visible quality loss

## Photorealism Cues

Strategic imperfections that bridge the uncanny valley and make generated images feel like real camera captures:

- **Lens smear**: Subtle softness in corners, mimics cheap or vintage glass
- **Light leak**: Warm orange/pink wash along edges, film camera artifact
- **Handshake blur**: Very slight motion blur suggesting handheld capture
- **Film grain**: Fine noise texture, especially visible in shadows
- **Dust particles**: Visible in backlit scenes, floating in light beams
- **Uneven exposure**: Slight vignetting or hot spots from natural light
- **Chromatic aberration**: Color fringing at high-contrast edges
- **Lens flare**: Light source artifacts, anamorphic streaks

**Usage**: Add 1-2 cues for subtle realism. Adding too many creates an obviously "filtered" look.

```
Professional photo with subtle handshake blur, slight lens vignette, natural grain
```

## Production Context Vocabulary

Terms used by creative directors, art directors, and production teams when briefing visual work:

- **Hero image**: The primary, full-bleed visual for a campaign, page, or feature
- **Key visual (KV)**: The central image that defines a campaign's look
- **Mood board frame**: A single reference image within a mood board collection
- **Thumbnail**: Small preview image, often for video or article selection
- **Editorial**: Magazine-quality imagery with narrative and styling intent
- **Campaign asset**: Any visual created for a specific marketing campaign
- **Packshot**: Clean product photo on neutral background, e-commerce standard
- **Lifestyle shot**: Product shown in real-world use context
- **Flat lay**: Overhead arrangement of objects on a surface
- **Lookbook**: Series of styled images showcasing a collection or range
- **Comp (comprehensive layout)**: Rough visual showing concept placement
- **Key art**: The definitive poster/image for a film, show, or event
- **B-roll frame**: Supporting visual that complements the hero

## Constraint Language

Vocabulary for controlling what should NOT appear in generated images. Essential for delta editing and precise generation:

### Exclusion Terms
- **"No text"**: Prevent any text, lettering, or typography from appearing
- **"No logos"**: Exclude brand marks, symbols, or identifiers
- **"Solo subject"**: Only one person/character in frame, no extras
- **"No people"**: Exclude all human figures
- **"No props"**: Clean scene without additional objects

### Preservation Terms (for editing)
- **"Keep X unchanged"**: Explicit instruction to preserve a specific element
- **"Subject stays [position]"**: Lock subject placement during background swaps
- **"Maintain lighting"**: Preserve light direction, quality, and color temperature
- **"Preserve composition"**: Keep framing, angles, and spatial relationships intact

### Boundary Terms
- **"Change ONLY [element]"**: Restrict modification to a single target
- **"Do not add"**: Prevent introduction of new elements
- **"Do not change"**: Protect specific elements from modification
- **"Match existing"**: New elements should conform to current scene properties

## Combining Terms Effectively

### Building with the 7-Element Formula
Layer elements systematically using Subject + Action + Setting + Style + Camera + Lighting + Constraints:

**1 element (subject only):**
```
A handmade ceramic coffee mug
```

**3 elements (subject + setting + lighting):**
```
A handmade ceramic coffee mug with crackle glaze on a rustic wooden table.
Soft morning window light.
```

**5 elements (+ style + camera):**
```
A handmade ceramic coffee mug with crackle glaze on a rustic wooden table.
Wabi-sabi aesthetic. 100mm macro, overhead angle.
Soft morning window light, warm tones.
```

**7 elements (+ action + constraints):**
```
A handmade ceramic coffee mug with crackle glaze, steam rising from fresh pour.
Rustic wooden table, farmhouse kitchen. Wabi-sabi aesthetic.
100mm macro, overhead angle. Soft morning window light, warm tones, serene.
No text. No other objects.
```

### Balancing Constraints
Don't over-constrain — allow creative interpretation:
- Good: "Impressionist style with visible brushstrokes and vibrant color palette"
- Too specific: "Exactly 47 visible brushstrokes in vermillion red with 15-degree angles"
